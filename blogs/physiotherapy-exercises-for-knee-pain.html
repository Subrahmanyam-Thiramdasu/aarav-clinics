Physiotherapy Exercises for Knee Pain: Strength, Mobility and Safety

Educational guide explaining common physiotherapy exercise types for knee pain, safe progression and warning signs.

Educational guide: This article provides general information and should not replace an individual assessment by a qualified physiotherapist.

Knee pain may be related to injury, overuse, reduced strength, osteoarthritis, post-surgical recovery or problems elsewhere in the leg. Exercises can be useful, but the correct programme depends on the cause of pain and the activities a person needs to return to.

This article explains common exercise categories used in physiotherapy. It should not replace an assessment, especially when the knee is swollen, unstable, locked or painful after an injury.

Why strengthening around the knee matters

The knee relies on muscles around the hip, thigh, calf and trunk to manage load during standing, walking, stairs and sport. Physiotherapy may focus on improving strength and movement control so daily tasks become easier and more confident.

Strengthening is progressed gradually. Performing difficult exercises before the knee can tolerate them may increase symptoms, while exercises that are too easy may not produce enough improvement.

Common knee exercise categories

  • Range-of-motion exercises.
  • Quadriceps strengthening.
  • Hip and gluteal strengthening.
  • Calf and hamstring exercises.
  • Balance and functional training.
  • Graded walking or cycling where appropriate.

Examples often used during rehabilitation

Depending on assessment findings, physiotherapists may prescribe quadriceps setting, straight-leg raises, supported knee bends, bridges, sit-to-stand practice, heel raises, step-ups or balance exercises. The programme should always be adapted to pain, swelling, strength and confidence.

How to monitor the knee after exercise

  • Observe whether swelling increases later that day or the following morning.
  • Watch for giving way, locking or reduced movement.
  • Reduce load if symptoms remain significantly worse after exercise.
  • Progress only one variable at a time.
  • Measure improvement using functional goals.
Seek prompt medical assessment after a significant injury, inability to bear weight, visible deformity, sudden locking, hot swollen joint with fever, new calf swelling, suspected fracture or rapidly worsening symptoms.

Why an individual plan is better than a generic routine

Two people with similar knee pain may have very different limitations. Assessment helps determine the correct starting point and allows exercises to be progressed safely according to recovery and goals.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are squats good for knee pain?

Squats can be useful when performed at an appropriate depth and load, but they are not suitable for every condition or stage of recovery.

Can I exercise if my knee is swollen?

Mild swelling may require a modified programme. Sudden or marked swelling should be medically assessed.

How long does knee physiotherapy take?

Recovery depends on the diagnosis, injury severity, surgery history, strength, goals and adherence to the rehabilitation programme.

Do knee exercises work for osteoarthritis?

Exercise and strengthening are commonly recommended to support pain management, mobility and function in knee osteoarthritis when individualised appropriately.
