OCCUPATIONAL THERAPY

What Is Occupational Therapy for Children?

Learn how pediatric occupational therapy supports daily activities, fine motor skills, sensory regulation, play, self-care and school participation.

Educational guide: This article is for educational purposes and should be clinically reviewed before publication.

Occupational therapy helps children participate in meaningful everyday activities such as play, dressing, eating, handwriting, classroom tasks, self-care, routines, social participation and age-appropriate independence.

The word occupation refers to meaningful daily activities—not only employment. Pediatric occupational therapy considers the child, the task and the environment to understand what is affecting participation.

What can pediatric occupational therapy support?

  • Fine motor skills such as grasping, drawing, cutting and manipulating small objects.
  • Self-care including dressing, feeding, toileting and grooming.
  • Sensory processing and regulation during daily routines.
  • Visual-motor and hand-eye coordination.
  • Play skills and peer participation.
  • School readiness, handwriting and classroom participation.
  • Planning, sequencing and completing multi-step tasks.
  • Adaptive strategies or equipment where appropriate.

What happens during an occupational therapy assessment?

The occupational therapist discusses the child's routines, strengths and challenges with the family. Assessment may include observation of play, fine motor skills, movement, sensory responses, self-care and school-related tasks. Standardized tools may also be used when appropriate.

What does therapy look like?

Therapy often looks like play because children learn best through active participation. Activities are selected to practice specific skills, adapt tasks or modify the environment. Sessions may include movement, hand skills, visual-motor activities, self-care practice and parent coaching.

How families are involved

Parents and caregivers play an essential role by sharing priorities and continuing practical strategies during everyday routines. Small changes at home can significantly support progress.

Occupational therapy in a multidisciplinary team

Children may also benefit from speech therapy, physiotherapy, early intervention, behavioral support and educational services. Coordinated care helps ensure goals complement each other.

When to consider an assessment

Consider an occupational therapy assessment if your child has persistent difficulty with self-care, fine motor skills, sensory regulation, classroom participation, play or daily routines.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is occupational therapy only for children with a diagnosis?

No. Referral may be based on functional challenges even without a formal diagnosis.

Does occupational therapy treat sensory issues?

Occupational therapists assess sensory processing as part of daily participation and recommend individualized strategies linked to functional goals.

Is handwriting the main focus of pediatric OT?

No. Pediatric occupational therapy also supports self-care, play, fine motor skills, visual-motor integration, sensory regulation and school participation.

How is progress measured?

Progress is measured through functional goals, parent and teacher feedback, clinical observations and selected assessment tools.
